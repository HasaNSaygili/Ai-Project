# Artist's Manifesto

## Echoes on the Threshold

*CSE 358 Introduction to Artificial Intelligence — Spring 2025–2026*

---

### Preface

I want to begin with a confession: I did not choose this song. The song chose the assignment, and the assignment chose me. When I first read "Knockin' on Heaven's Door" as the starting point for this project, I felt the particular discomfort of something that is too close to be looked at directly. Not because I knew the song well — I did not, at first — but because the question it asks is one I had been avoiding for a long time. What do you carry that you can no longer use? What would it mean to put it down?

I did not immediately sit down and build something. Before I wrote a single line of code, I spent time with the song. I listened to the original recording from the *Pat Garrett & Billy the Kid* soundtrack. I watched the film. I read about Dylan's life in 1973 — the pressure he was under, the deliberate retreat from the spotlight he had engineered throughout the late 1960s, the way he had moved to the country and tried, with limited success, to become a private person again. And then I read about the Vietnam War, which was in its final, agonizing chapter in 1973, and about the generation of young Americans who had come home from it — not as heroes, but as people trying to figure out what to do with the weight they had carried back.

All of that research found its way into this project. This manifesto is an attempt to explain how, and why.

---

### Why This Medium

I am a computer science student. My instinct, when given a creative assignment, is to build something. Not to write about something, not to analyze something, but to make a system that does something — that responds, reacts, and changes based on what you give it.

But I wanted to build something that was not just technically interesting. I wanted to build something that did what art does: create a space where a person can encounter themselves unexpectedly. A space with some resistance in it. A space that asks something back.

An interactive web experience seemed like the right container for this. Not a static image, not a generated audio file you download and play in the background. Something you walk into. Something that changes based on how you respond to it. The door metaphor was already in the song — it felt natural, almost inevitable, to make the experience literally about doors. About knocking. About waiting to see what opens.

The web format made possible something that mattered to me: the work could be experienced alone, in the dark, with headphones. There is a quality of intimacy in that situation that a gallery installation or a classroom demo cannot replicate. The song itself has that quality — it is not a performance piece, it is a private moment. Dylan is not singing to a crowd in that film. He is singing to a dying man. He is present at a threshold, and the song is what he found to say.

I wanted the experience to have that same private quality. Something that happens between the person and themselves, with the machine as a kind of witness. Not public. Not demonstrative. Not a product, but a threshold.

### What Caught Me: The Badge

I knew nothing about the film before I began researching. *Pat Garrett & Billy the Kid* (Sam Peckinpah, 1973) is a revisionist Western about friendship, loyalty, betrayal, and the death of an era. Pat Garrett, once an outlaw, has become a lawman — he has taken the badge, accepted the authority of the powerful, and been hired to hunt down his old friend Billy the Kid. It is a film about what happens to a man when he trades his identity for security and then has to live with what that trade cost him.

The song appears in the scene where Sheriff Baker, shot and dying slowly, asks his wife to take his badge off of him. He cannot use it anymore. Dylan — who wrote the score for the film and appears in it as a character — gives those words to the dying sheriff: *Mama, take this badge off of me. I can't use it anymore.*

What caught me was not the dying. It was the badge.

The badge is an object, but it is also an identity. It is the thing that tells you and everyone else who you are, what you are for, what you are permitted to do. The sheriff is not just dying — he is, for the first time, becoming a person without a role. The badge has defined him. Without it, stripped of it, who is he?

This question — who are you when the thing that defined you is gone? — is not a question that belongs only to 1973. But 1973 gave it a particular historical weight that I found impossible to ignore.

### The Historical Weight of 1973

To understand what the song meant when it was written, you have to understand what 1973 felt like — not as an abstraction, but as a lived experience for a specific generation of Americans.

The Vietnam War had been consuming American lives for more than a decade. By 1973, with the signing of the Paris Peace Accords in January, American military involvement was formally ending — but the ending did not feel like victory. It felt like exhaustion. It felt like the slow recognition that something large and destructive had been done, that tens of thousands of people had died, that a country on the other side of the world had been devastated, and that the reasons given had been, at best, insufficient. The young men who had fought — many of them drafted, given no choice about whether to carry the weapon — were coming home to a country that did not know how to look at them. They were not celebrated. They were not condemned. They were simply returned to civilian life and expected to act as though nothing unusual had happened.

What were they supposed to do with what they had carried?

This is the question that the counterculture had been asking, in different forms, since the mid-1960s: the protests, the draft card burnings, the marches, the refusals. The counterculture was, in part, a collective act of removing the badge. A generation saying, loudly and often at significant personal cost: *I cannot use this anymore. I do not accept the identity you have handed me. I refuse the uniform, the authority, the role.*

By 1973, the counterculture itself was fading. Its energy had dissipated. Woodstock was four years in the past. The idealism of the 1960s had been tested against reality and had survived only partially. What remained was something quieter and sadder — not rebellion, but the slow, private work of figuring out how to live after the revolution that was not quite a revolution.

Dylan's song is a document of that moment. It is not angry. It is not triumphant. It is, in every sense of the word, tired. *It's getting dark, too dark to see. I feel I'm knockin' on heaven's door.* There is no protest in those words. There is only the recognition that something is ending, and the need to put down what you have been carrying before you go through the door.

Sam Peckinpah, who directed the film, understood this intimately. He was himself a man out of time in 1973 — a filmmaker whose brutal, elegiac Westerns were eulogies for a mythology of American masculinity that was dying. *Pat Garrett & Billy the Kid* is, among other things, a film about men who cannot survive the end of the era they were built for. Garrett lives, but he lives as a collaborator, a man who sold his authentic self for security. Billy dies, but he dies as himself. The film does not tell you which of them you should envy.

The song outlived its original context, as Dylan's work tends to do. In 1997, Thomas Jahn's film *Knocking on Heaven's Door* borrowed the title and the feeling — two terminally ill strangers who escape the hospital and drive toward the sea, trying to live fully in the days they have left. The film has nothing to do with Dylan, the Western, or Vietnam. Yet the song fits it perfectly. This is what caught my attention most: the song is not about 1973. It is about the moment — any moment, any person, any era — when the weight becomes too much and the door seems close. The historical context gives the song its specific gravity. But the question it asks is not historical. It is permanent.

I thought about this a great deal while building the project. The four symbolic doors in the experience — Moon, Flame, Feather, Key — are not arbitrary. They are distillations of the emotional territories that the song and its era mapped: longing and grief (the soldiers who did not come back, the friends lost to the war or to the distance that the war created); rage and defiance (the counterculture, the refusal, the burning); release and acceptance (the ceasefire, the laying down of the weapon, the badge given to the wife); and the weight of the past (the classified missions, the things that were done that cannot be undone, the names carried and never spoken).

The NLP model that reads a user's answers and determines which door opens does not know any of this history. But the anchor texts I gave it — the phrases that define each emotional territory — are written in the language of that history. "Laying down the weapon. The badge you can no longer use. The soldiers who did not come back. The war that was wrong." The model finds the direction of your words in relation to those anchors. It places you in the emotional landscape of 1973 without telling you that is what it is doing.

### How the Historical Context Became Structure

The challenge with historical context as a creative requirement is that it can easily become decoration. A reference. A mood board. Sepia filters and typewriter fonts. I was determined not to let that happen. The 1973 context had to be structural, not cosmetic — embedded in the logic of the system itself, not painted over the surface.

I did this in four ways.

**First, the questions.** The question bank the experience draws from was built specifically around the themes of that era and that song. Some questions ask directly about war, about orders followed under doubt, about badges worn and weapons carried: *"You were handed a weapon before you understood what carrying it would cost. Did you ever truly choose it?"* Others approach the same territory obliquely, through the universal experiences that 1973 gave a specific historical shape: loss, complicity, identity, the silence that makes you an accomplice. The questions are designed to make the user feel the weight of the threshold without explaining where the weight comes from.

**Second, the LLM system prompt.** The poem generation module does not receive a generic instruction to "write a poem." It receives a historically grounded context: Dylan's voice, the dying sheriff, the Vietnam era, the counterculture's exhaustion, the specific quality of 1973's tiredness. The prompt instructs the model to write in the tradition of that moment — sparse, honest, direct, without comfort or decoration. The poems it generates carry that instruction in their bones, whether or not the user who receives them knows where it comes from.

**Third, the NLP anchor texts.** The four emotional territories that the Universal Sentence Encoder uses as reference points are written in the language of 1973. They are not generic emotional categories — they are specific to the historical and cultural context of the song. "Feather" is not just peace and acceptance; it is ceasefire, armistice, laying down the weapon, the badge you can no longer use. "Flame" is not just anger; it is protest, draft refusal, the counterculture's defiant no. The machine does not know this history, but the language it measures against carries it.

**Fourth, the visual and sonic aesthetic.** The sepia palette, the film grain, the CRT scanlines, the amber light leaking under the door, the low drone that underpins every transition — these are not decorative choices. They are an argument: that the past is present. That when you sit down to answer these questions, you are not doing so in 2025. You are doing so at the threshold. And the threshold does not have a year.

### AI as What

The project requirement asks: was AI a tool, a collaborator, a mirror, or something else entirely?

My honest answer is that the two AI systems in this project play two completely different roles — and that the difference between them matters.

The **NLP component** — the Universal Sentence Encoder, a deep learning model that encodes text as 512-dimensional vectors in semantic embedding space — is a listener. It does not generate anything. It reads. When you type your answer to a question, it converts your words into a point in a vast semantic space and measures how close that point is to four reference territories. It does not judge your answer. It does not decide what it means. It distributes the weight of your words across four directions and passes that distribution forward.

This is, in a strange way, the most human thing in the system. It is the act of being heard — not understood, not analyzed, but received. Your words arrive somewhere. They have a direction. They mean something, even if you are not sure what. There is no interpretation yet. There is only the reading.

Crucially, the system does not just pass the *winning* door to the next stage. It passes the full probability distribution — all four scores, weighted precisely: "Moon 72%, Flame 18%, Feather 6%, Key 4%." This distinction matters. A soul that is 72% longing and 18% rage is carrying something fundamentally different from a soul that is 51% longing and 44% rage, even though both would be assigned to the Moon door. The full distribution is the emotional fingerprint. That fingerprint is what the poem responds to.

The **LLM component** — Llama 3.1, accessed through Groq's inference API — is an interpreter. It receives the full emotional map produced across all three rounds, along with the actual text of the answers, and it writes a poem. Not a summary. Not an analysis. A poem, in Dylan's tradition: sparse language, no decoration, direct address. It speaks to you — not about you.

Together, the two systems do something that neither could do alone. The NLP layer makes the experience feel heard — it creates the sense that something is reading you accurately, picking up on what you were reaching toward even when you were not precise. The LLM layer makes the experience feel spoken to — it creates the sense that something understood what you were carrying and found the right words for it. The first reads. The second speaks. Between them, there is something that resembles a conversation.

I did not expect to feel that when I was building it. I thought it would feel like engineering. It felt more like watching two instruments find the same key.

And then the third element: the voice. The Web Speech API reads the poem aloud at the lowest possible pitch and slowest comfortable rate, while a five-layer harmonic drone — sub-bass at 27.5 Hz, fundamental at 55 Hz, fifth at 82.5 Hz, octave, shimmer — fills the space around it through a ten-second convolution reverb. The TTS voice cannot be routed through the Web Audio engine. But the drone creates the acoustic illusion that the voice is resonating in a vast stone chamber — the way a dying man's last words might sound in a cathedral, or at the edge of a canyon in New Mexico, in 1973.

### My Door

What does "knocking on heaven's door" mean in my own life?

I will try to be honest, which means I will try not to be literary.

I have spent most of my life performing competence. Performing certainty. Performing the kind of confidence that is expected of someone in a technical field — someone who is supposed to know things, to have answers, to solve problems efficiently and without visible confusion. I carried this performance the way the sheriff carried the badge. Not because it was forced on me, but because I put it on willingly, and after a while I forgot I was wearing it. It stopped feeling like a performance and started feeling like who I was.

This is, I have come to think, a very common experience among people in technical fields. The badge in this case is not a sheriff's star — it is the identity of the person who knows, who can fix it, who does not need to ask for help. It is worn for good reasons: it provides structure, it earns respect, it creates a sense of forward motion. But it also creates distance. Between you and the work. Between you and the people around you. Between you and whatever it is you actually feel about any of it.

This project forced me to spend several weeks thinking about thresholds. About what it means to reach the end of something — a war, a role, a version of yourself — and have to decide what you take with you through the door. The sheriff in the film takes nothing. He hands the badge to his wife. He stops being a sheriff and becomes, for the first time, just a person. Just himself.

There is something terrifying in that image. And there is also something that I found, unexpectedly, to be a relief.

I do not think I am ready to lay down the badge I wear. I am not even sure I can see it clearly enough yet to describe its shape. But this project made me sit with the question seriously, perhaps for the first time — not as a concept, not as a philosophical exercise, but as a feeling. The kind of feeling that arrives when you are alone, in the dark, with headphones, and something speaks to you in a voice that is too low to ignore.

That is what the song does, if you let it work on you. It finds you at whatever threshold you happen to be standing at and asks: what did you come here carrying? What are you ready to put down?

I built this experience hoping it would ask that question to other people. I think it asked it to me first. I think that was the point.

### A Final Note

Dylan accepted the Nobel Prize for Literature in 2016. In his acceptance lecture, delivered in Stockholm by proxy, he spoke about what it means to make something — a song, a story — that contains more than you put into it. He spoke about songs being alive, about how they do not stay fixed, about how they find new meanings in the mouths of new singers and the ears of new listeners, across decades and languages and lives that the original songwriter could never have imagined.

"Songs are alive in the land of the living," he said. "They are not just something to feel. They direct your feelings."

I am a computer science student. I make systems. But I have come to believe, through this project, that a well-designed system can direct feelings too. Not manipulate them. Not simulate them. Direct them — the way a question asked at the right moment, in the right voice, can open something in a person that has been closed for a long time.

The machine does not feel anything. The NLP model does not know it is reading a human soul. The LLM does not know it is writing for a specific person. The drone does not know it is creating the acoustic space for a revelation. None of them know. But together, aimed carefully, they create the conditions for something real to happen in the person who sits in front of them.

That is what I was trying to build. Whether I succeeded is not for me to say.

Knock.

The door is yours to design. What's behind it is yours to discover.

### AI Tools & Models Used

In accordance with the academic integrity requirements of this assignment, all AI tools, models, and APIs used in this project are listed below.

| Tool / Model | Role in the project |
|---|---|
| **TensorFlow.js 4.17.0** | Browser-side ML runtime for running the NLP model |
| **Universal Sentence Encoder 1.3.3** (Google) | Converts user text to 512-dimensional embeddings; cosine similarity determines which door opens |
| **Groq API** (inference endpoint) | Fast LLM inference hosting |
| **Llama-3.1-8b-instant** (Meta) | Generates the personalized poem from the user's emotional journey map |
| **Web Speech API** (browser-native) | Text-to-speech synthesis for reading the poem aloud |
| **Web Audio API** (browser-native) | 5-layer harmonic drone (A0 sub-bass → A3 shimmer) through 10-second convolution reverb; creates the cathedral acoustic space beneath the voice |

No generative AI tool was used to write this manifesto or to design the creative concept. All architectural decisions, the question bank, the door anchor texts, the LLM system prompt, and the philosophical framing of the experience are original work.
